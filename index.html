A Grant Writing Workshop

How to Lose a Grant

A Field Guide to Getting It Wrong

A reverse guide to the most common mistakes that send proposals straight to the rejection pile, and what to do instead.

1
Mistake No. 1

Start writing the day before it's due.

"The deadline is tomorrow at 5pm. Plenty of time. You work well under pressure. You've always worked well under pressure. The logic model can't be that hard."

The Flip Side
  • Build your timeline backwards from the deadline
  • Budget at least 2 weeks for internal review and sign-off
  • Treat your first draft as a starting point, not a final product
2
Mistake No. 2

Skip reading the RFP. It's probably just boilerplate.

"Your literacy program is excellent. The RFP was for environmental conservation. Close enough. You do use paper."

The Flip Side
  • Read it once for eligibility, once for priorities
  • Highlight every requirement and check each one off before submitting
  • If you're forcing your project to fit, it probably doesn't fit
3
Mistake No. 3

Write it so only you understand it.

"Our CBPR-informed SDOH intervention leverages an FQHC-adjacent MTSS framework to address SDoH gaps in our CBO's target ZIP." Nailed it.

The Flip Side
  • Define every acronym the first time you use it
  • Read your draft out loud. If you stumble, rewrite it
  • Ask someone outside your field to read it before it goes in
4
Mistake No. 4

Make the budget a rough guess.

"Personnel: $40,000. Supplies: $10,000. Other: $5,000. The 'Other' line covers everything else. Don't worry about it."

The Flip Side
  • Every line item needs a calculation behind it, not a feeling
  • Your budget and your work plan should tell the exact same story
  • Write the budget narrative like the reviewer will read it, because they will
5
Mistake No. 5

Promise outcomes you can't possibly measure.

"By project end, participants will have experienced profound personal transformation and a renewed sense of civic hope."

The Flip Side
  • Name the specific change you expect to see, in who, by when
  • Identify your measurement tool before you write the outcome
  • If you can't measure it, it's a goal, not an outcome
6
Mistake No. 6

Let one person write the whole thing alone.

"Karen knows grants. Karen will handle it. Karen has not slept since Tuesday."

The Flip Side
  • Loop in program staff, finance, and leadership early
  • Community partners should shape the narrative, not just sign letters
  • Build in a review round with fresh eyes before submission
7
Mistake No. 7

Treat the funder like an ATM.

"Dear Sir or Madam, Please find enclosed our proposal. We believe you fund things like this. Regards."

The Flip Side
  • Research their last three years of funded grants
  • Contact the program officer before the deadline if they allow it
  • Frame your project around their mission, not just your need
8
Mistake No. 8

Ignore the page limit and formatting requirements.

"The limit said 10 pages. You submitted 24. In Comic Sans. The margins are half an inch. You felt the work deserved more space."

The Flip Side
  • Build a submission checklist from the RFP requirements
  • Check font size, margins, file format, and attachment limits
  • Have someone else run the checklist. You'll miss what you're used to seeing
9
Mistake No. 9

Assume the reviewer already knows why this matters.

"The need for this program is well established and widely understood." [No citations. No data. No further explanation.]

The Flip Side
  • Open with local data, not organizational history
  • Name the specific gap your project addresses
  • Let a community member's voice make the case, not just your statistics
10
Mistake No. 10

Submit and forget.

"You won. You spent the money. You filed no interim report. The funder emailed twice. You'll get to it."

The Flip Side
  • Send a thank-you to the program officer within a week
  • Request reviewer feedback whether you win or lose
  • Report your outcomes even when no one is requiring you to
11
Mistake No. 11

Copy last year's proposal and change the date.

"Everything still applies. Probably. The executive director whose bio is on page 8 retired in April but she was great so let's leave her in."

The Flip Side
  • Update every staff bio, statistic, and program description
  • Check whether the funder's priorities have shifted since last cycle
  • A refreshed proposal shows you're still paying attention
12
Mistake No. 12

Write the needs statement about your organization, not the community.

"Our department has been chronically underfunded since 2018. We deserve this grant. The community section starts on page 9 but honestly we ran out of space."

The Flip Side
  • Lead with community data, not organizational history
  • The funder is investing in the people you serve, not your department
  • Save your capacity story for the organizational background section
13
Mistake No. 13

Include every program you've ever run as proof of capacity.

"Appendix A through Q covers our full organizational history. Appendix R is a photo of our 2011 staff retreat. We thought it showed culture."

The Flip Side
  • Include only what directly supports this proposal
  • Two strong, relevant examples beat ten generic ones
  • Appendices should add evidence, not bulk
14
Mistake No. 14

Use the grant to fund something you were already going to do anyway.

"We call it innovation. Accountants call it something else. The site visitor is scheduled for March."

The Flip Side
  • Funders expect to see genuine additionality
  • Be honest about what the grant makes possible that wouldn't happen otherwise
  • If everything would happen anyway, find a better fit
15
Mistake No. 15

Get a letter of support from your own board chair addressed to yourself.

"Dear [Our Organization], We at [Our Organization's Board] are pleased to support [Our Organization] in this exciting initiative. Sincerely, [Person Who Works Down The Hall]."

The Flip Side
  • Letters should come from community partners, not internal leadership
  • A strong letter names the specific partnership, not just general support
  • One specific letter beats five generic ones every time
16
Mistake No. 16

Describe your evaluation plan in one sentence.

"We will measure success using a variety of appropriate tools and methods to be determined at a later date as the project evolves organically."

The Flip Side
  • Name who will collect data, what they will collect, and how often
  • Connect each outcome to a specific measurement method
  • Your evaluation plan shows funders you plan to be accountable
Now that we know how to lose one...

Let's talk about how to win.

The mistakes are easy to laugh at. The specifics that follow are harder to find. These are the things most grant writing guides skip — and the things reviewers notice most.

Pro Tip
Often Overlooked No. 1

The funder's last 10 grants are your best research tool.

Most applicants read the funder's website. Savvy applicants look up what they actually funded on GuideStar, their 990, or their grants database. The difference between what a funder says they care about and what they actually write checks for is often very revealing.

Pro Tip
Often Overlooked No. 2

Your needs statement should make the reviewer uncomfortable.

Not manipulative. Specific. If a reviewer finishes your needs statement thinking "well, that's a shame," you haven't done your job. They should feel the urgency of acting now, in this community, with this organization.

Pro Tip
Often Overlooked No. 3

The strongest proposals borrow credibility.

Letters from elected officials, citations from peer-reviewed research, endorsements from recognized community institutions. You don't have to be the most credible voice in the room. You just have to surround yourself with people who are.

Pro Tip
Often Overlooked No. 4

Name your project.

"The Literacy Initiative" is forgettable. "Reading Ready" is not. A named project signals that you've thought this through, makes the work feel real, and gives the funder something to point to when they talk about their portfolio.

Pro Tip
Often Overlooked No. 5

Write the evaluation section like a skeptic will read it.

Assume the reviewer doesn't believe your outcomes are achievable. Your job is to show them the methodology that makes it plausible. Don't ask them to trust you. Show them why they should.

Pro Tip
Often Overlooked No. 6

A declined proposal is a first draft of your next submission.

Request reviewer comments every time. Most funders will share them. Applicants who treat rejection as feedback get funded faster than those who treat it as a verdict.

Pro Tip
Often Overlooked No. 7

Indirect costs are not optional. They're an argument for sustainability.

Many applicants leave indirect costs out to look lean. Experienced reviewers see that as a red flag. If your organization can't cover its overhead from this grant, the question becomes: where is that money coming from?

Pro Tip
Often Overlooked No. 8

The cover letter is not a formality.

One page. Lead with the ask, name the program, connect it to the funder's stated priorities, and close with a specific next step. Reviewers often read the cover letter before they read anything else. Make it count.
